## Ornamentation
Rankin's upbringing as part of a family of Celtic folk musicians has clearly had some influence on the band's guitar writing - while she has mentioned the more general influence of Celtic melodies on her vocal melodic writing, there are a few places where this influence comes through particularly strongly. Rankin grew up learning the fiddle and it is likely that this had at least some impact on how the band approaches guitar lead writing. 
Celtic fiddle music makes heavy use of ornamentation - additional notes which are used to embellish an otherwise simple melody. One type of ornament is the mordent, which requires you to play the written note, quickly move up to the note above (or sometimes below), then return again to the original note. ## Influences
It's no secret that the intro to Hey is a direct homage to Stereolab's Emperor Tomato Ketchup; the song's steady motorik drum pattern  (a favourite of Stereolab), pulsating bass line, and sonic embellishments produced by treating synthesisers as sound effect generators rather than melodic instruments are all as clearly recognisable in Alvvays’ take as they are in the original. However, while Alvvays is a band known for wearing their influences on their sleeves, many of their musical references are a little less direct.
Kevin Shields of shoegaze-pioneering band My Bloody Valentine is credited as the inventor of the technique dubbed "glide guitar", which involves keeping hold of the instrument's tremolo arm (or whammy bar) while strumming chords, creating a swaying vibrato-like effect as the strings are subtly bent in and out of tune by the arm's movement. Conveniently, both of guitarist Alec O'Hanley's main instruments of choice, a Fender Jaguar and Rickenbacker 360, are equipped with a tremolo arm, allowing him to execute the technique in a few of the band's songs. In Undertow and Underneath Us, their two most blatantly shoegaze-influenced tracks, both make heavy use of the technique, with O'Hanley's strumming submerged in fuzzy distortion in line with the characteristic shoegaze sound.  The technique is also found, albeit with a much cleaner guitar sound, in Dreams Tonite - the steady 8th note strumming pattern, paired with a warbly chorus effect, bears a strong resemblance to Cherry Coloured Funk by Cocteau Twins, a band that Alvvays have repeatedly cited as an influence .

## Instruments and Gear
A discussion of the guitar sound of Alvvays would not be complete without at least a brief mention of the equipment they use to channel their jangly riffs and driving chords . Their generous use of delay and reverb, particularly on their self-titled debut album, contributes to their frequent categorisation as “dream pop”. However, a lot of their signature guitar tone, particularly in live performances, is owed to the particular type of delay they use. Rankin’s selection of guitar pedals includes an MXR Carbon Copy, whereas O’Hanley’s pedalboard features a vintage Electro-Harmonix Deluxe Memory Man ; both of these are analog delay pedals with a modulation feature. 
Analog delays are associated with a darker, muddier sound than other types of delay, producing a trail of echoes that tend to blend into one another, rather than producing distinct repeats of the original notes being played. This results in an effect that in many cases can function more like reverb than delay, in contrast to the more rhythmic effect of clearer, brighter delay pedals. “Modulation” refers to a subtle, periodic variation of the pedal’s delay time (i.e. the time between each individual echo), adding a chorus-like effect to the delay’s echoes. The result is more subtle than directly applying a dedicated chorus effect, but nevertheless adds an extra dimension to the wash of echoes that accompany many of their guitar parts.
Although she has previously played other guitars such as the Fender Mustang and Jag-Stang (a mashup of Fender’s Mustang and Jaguar, designed by Kurt Cobain), Rankin’s long-standing instrument of choice is a 1961 Fender Duo-Sonic. According to Rankin:
I have a 60s Duo-Sonic that I always come back to no matter what guitar I’m maybe periodically attracted to. It sounds the same no matter what you play it through, and I’m fond of that sound. I think it’s become a part of the way our records and our band sound.
The Duo-Sonic certainly has a characteristic tone that is well suited to jangle pop; its sound is fairly bright and treble-focused, as opposed to other guitars with a more full-bodied tone. Meanwhile, one of O’Hanley’s main guitars is a Rickenbacker, a brand that has been associated with jangle since the 60s when Roger McGuinn picked up a selection of their guitars to play as frontman of The Byrds. O’Hanley’s other main guitar, a Fender Jaguar, has a long-standing association with alternative and indie music of the 80s and 90s, especially shoegaze.
It is also worth noting that all these guitars are short-scale instruments, with scale lengths shorter than the standard of 25.5 inches adopted for the majority of guitars made by Fender and other manufacturers. Scale length refers to the total length of the guitar string that vibrates to produce sound; a shorter scale length means strings will be under less tension, resulting in a noticeable difference in feel and sound to their full-scale counterparts.
